Everyday
Antifascism

Depending on your choice of news sources, antifascists, or “antifa” (or Antifa, Anti-fa, even the needlessly acronymic ANTIFA) have been portrayed as violent, unhinged radicals, hell-bent on destruction of the rights of others. Or, at the other end of the spectrum, as a loose network of far-left activists taking action to defend their communities against individuals and organizations who would do them great political and social harm.

In practice the latter interpretation is closest to the truth, though violence has certainly played a role in the history of antifascist activism. Struggles against colonialism, resistance to the KKK, partisan groups during World War II, and the evolution of leftist actions since then—all have incorporated violent activity to some degree or another.



What isn’t as well-documented are the non-violent activites of these activists.












Not everyone can or wants to physically engage with their political opposites, nor is such engagement necessarily a winning strategy. Historically, conservative and far-right entities have held a monopoly on violence due to a variety of factors. Standing up to their oppressive politics requires careful research, clear communication, social exposure, solid support, and comprehensive education. These are elements that historian and author Mark Bray describes as “Everyday Antifascism” in his book Antifa: The Anti Fascist Handbook.

These non-violent tactics are the focus of this web site. The site is not to be considered as the end-all of antifascist activism, more a brief summation of tactics and the start of a counter-narrative against the notion that activists of this nature are violent and unhinged, but instead everyday people working to defend themselves and their communities against political and social oppression.

This site leans heavily on Mark Bray’s book for both inspiration and content, especially its Chapter Six, “Strategy, (Non)Violence, and Everyday Antifascism”.

The book can be found via its publisher, Melville House.
